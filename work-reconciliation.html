    /01 Setting the Stage

    Every payment leaves more than one trail.

    Reconciliation is cross-checking two ledgers to make sure they agree. It runs in two layers — each comparing exactly two sources at a time, not all four together.

    Layer 1 asks: does what we recorded in OMS match what Adyen actually settled? Layer 2 asks: does what Adyen sent match what the bank received? When a layer finds a gap, an exception is raised — and this case study is about the workspace built for the people who resolve them.

    /02 What's an Exception

    One mismatch can hide sixteen different stories.

    When the two sides don't agree, it becomes an exception that needs manual review. There are six ways that can happen — and each one implies a different resolution.

    On top of the reason, the shape of the records involved varies too. Transactions don't always map one-to-one — a single order can split into several settlement lines, or a batch settlement can bundle dozens of orders into one bank entry.

    →
    One-to-one
    A single order, a single settlement line. The expected case — and the one most tools assume by default.
    →
    One-to-many
    One order splits into several settlement entries — a capture, a fee, a refund — each landing separately.
    →
    Many-to-one
    Several orders settle together as a single bank entry — typically from end-of-day batching.
    →
    Many-to-many
    A batch of orders settles against a batch of bank entries, with no fixed pairing between them.

    Pair six mismatch types with four record shapes, and an analyst is effectively triaging across sixteen distinct situations — every one of them initially presented the same way: "this didn't match."

    /03 Where Analysts Get Stuck

    The data lines up. The workflow doesn't.

    Today, resolving one exception means moving across tools — exporting a report from the payments dashboard, pulling a settlement file from the bank portal, cross-referencing transaction IDs in a spreadsheet, and writing the outcome back somewhere else entirely.

    None of these steps are hard on their own. Repeated dozens of times a day, across exceptions that all look identical at a glance, the problem isn't difficulty — it's friction.

    • No single view of what's broken, why, or how many
    • Every exception type implies a different resolution — but the spreadsheet doesn't know that
    • Context — the original order, the settlement line, the customer — sits in three different systems
    • Decisions and reasoning are recorded manually, if at all, with no audit trail
    Design Challenge

    Give every exception — whatever its shape or reason — the same starting point: what was expected, what's different, and what can be done about it.

    /04 Designing the Structure

    One structure, however the transaction splits.

    Designing a separate screen for every combination of mismatch reason and record shape would mean designing for all of the permutations above — and then some. Instead, we anchored the design around three things that hold true for every exception, no matter how many records sit on either side.

    01
    Context
    What was expected — pulled straight from the original transaction: order, amount, parties, timestamps.
    02
    Delta
    What's actually different — the one thing the analyst needs to see first, however many records it spans.
    03
    Action
    The resolution options valid for this exception's type — never a generic "edit" button.

    With this anchor in place, a one-to-many exception — one order, three settlement lines — and a many-to-many exception — fifty orders settling against one bank entry — can share the same screen. Only the Context and Delta panels resize to fit; the structure around them stays exactly where the analyst learned to expect it.
