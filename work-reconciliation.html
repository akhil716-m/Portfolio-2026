    /01 Setting the Stage

    Every movement of money leaves more than one trail.

    As money moves through different systems, each one independently records its own version of the same event.

    OMS
    Merchant records it
    →
    PSP (Adyen)
    Provider settles it

    Bank

    A merchant records what was initiated, a payment provider records what was settled, and a bank records what was eventually received.

    Reconciliation is the process of verifying that these records stay in sync. When they do, money moves quietly through the system. When they don't, an exception is created for investigation.

    /02 What Makes Exceptions Difficult

    An exception is only a signal, not the answer.

    The system can tell when two records disagree, but it can't immediately explain why. The same exception can represent entirely different problems, and each one requires a different way of investigating and resolving it.

    Some mismatches are straightforward — a number doesn't match. Others are more nuanced, like a missing record, flipped balance directions, or inconsistent metadata.

    Before an analyst can fix anything, they first need to understand what exactly went wrong.

    /03 Why Investigation Becomes Difficult

    One mismatch can hide many different stories.

    Knowing why two records disagree is only part of the picture. Analysts also need to understand how those records relate to each other.

    Transactions don't always map one-to-one. A single order can split into multiple settlement lines, while multiple orders can eventually settle into a single bank entry.

    That means two exceptions with the same mismatch reason can have completely different structures underneath.

    Before taking any action, analysts first need to reconstruct how records are connected.

    →
    One-to-one
    A single order, a single settlement line. The expected case — and the one most tools assume by default.
    →
    One-to-many
    One order splits into several settlement entries — a capture, a fee, a refund — each landing separately.
    →
    Many-to-one
    Several orders settle together as a single bank entry — typically from end-of-day batching.
    →
    Many-to-many
    A batch of orders settles against a batch of bank entries, with no fixed pairing between them.
    An analyst isn't simply fixing mismatches—they're rebuilding the story behind how records relate to one another.
    /04 Design Challenge

    Analysts needed more than an exception label.

    An exception already tells analysts that something is wrong. But taking action requires much more context.

    They need to understand who is being compared and what is actually being compared underneath. The two can vary independently, making the same exception appear deceptively simple.

    Without that visibility, analysts are forced to manually reconstruct relationships before they can confidently act.

    Why analysts get stuck

    • The exception label hides what's happening underneath.
    • Resolution depends on entry-level relationships, not account-level labels.
    • Analysts must reconstruct missing context manually.
    • The same mismatch can require entirely different actions.

    Design challenge

    Make the hidden structure visible before investigation begins.

    The interface needed to reveal both who was being compared and what was actually being compared underneath.

    /05 Designing the Investigation

    Every exception needed the same questions answered.

    Designing a separate experience for every mismatch type and transaction shape would quickly become unmanageable.

    Instead, we identified the few questions analysts always needed answered, regardless of whether an exception involved one record or fifty.

    Rather than adapting the interface to every scenario, we kept the investigation structure consistent and allowed the information inside it to adapt to the complexity.

    01
    What happened?
    Surface the discrepancy in plain language before exposing raw records.
    02
    Where is the difference?
    Highlight exactly what's missing, extra, or mismatched.
    03
    What can I do next?
    Offer only the actions that are valid for this exception.
    Regardless of how many records were involved, analysts always returned to the same investigation flow. The information adapted to the complexity—not the interface.
    /06 From Investigation to Action

    Resolving an exception wasn't the difficult part. Understanding it was.

    Before analysts could take any action, they first had to answer a series of questions for every exception.

    • What transaction is this about?
    • Which accounts are involved?
    • Which entries sit underneath?
    • What exactly went wrong?
    • And finally, what action should be taken?

    None of this information was unavailable. It already existed within the reconciliation system. The problem was that analysts had to reconstruct this context manually, every single time.

    While this effort might seem small for a single exception, it quickly becomes expensive when analysts are reviewing dozens of exceptions every day.

    The design goal was straightforward: reduce the effort required to understand an exception before asking analysts to resolve it.

    To achieve this, the experience was built around two simple stages.

    1. Understand the exception
    Bring hidden information to the surface so analysts can immediately understand the story behind the exception.

    2. Resolve the exception
    Once the context is clear, guide analysts toward the appropriate action with confidence.

    Hero / The Workspace A single workspace that brings together the information analysts need to move from understanding to resolution.

    01 / Understand the exception

    The interface surfaces the information analysts repeatedly searched for during investigations.

    Participating accounts, underlying entries, matching rules, timestamps, topology, and the reason behind the mismatch are all brought forward before analysts interact with the raw records.

    Instead of rebuilding context manually, they can immediately understand what this exception is about.

    01 / Understand the Exception Account-level and entry-level context surfaced upfront — participating accounts, underlying entries, matching rules, and the reason behind the mismatch.

    02 / Resolve the exception

    Once the exception is understood, taking action becomes significantly easier.

    The interface highlights exactly where the disagreement exists and surfaces only the actions that are relevant to that exception.

    Analysts can focus on resolving the issue instead of figuring out how to resolve it.

    02 / Resolve the Exception OMS and PSP account entries side by side — mismatches highlighted, missing entries surfaced, and resolution actions like Match Entry available in context.

    03 / The many faces of an exception

    A single exception type can surface in very different shapes depending on how the transaction was structured.

    One-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-one, many-to-many, missing entries — each permutation demands a different investigation path, yet the interface resolves them all through one consistent structure.
