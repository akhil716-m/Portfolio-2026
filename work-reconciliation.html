    /01 Setting the Stage

    Every payment leaves more than one trail.

    Reconciliation ensures these records stay in sync as money moves across systems.

    A single payment creates multiple records — what we recorded internally, what the payment provider settled, and what the bank ultimately received.

    Layer 2

    compare →
    Bank

    →
    Match or Exception
    If they agree → matched ✓

    Rather than comparing everything at once, the system validates these records layer by layer. Whenever two records disagree, an exception is created for investigation.

    Exceptions are the starting point of the investigation experience designed for finance operations teams to identify, understand, and resolve them.

    /03 The Many Shapes of a Mismatch

    One mismatch can hide sixteen different stories.

    The reason behind an exception is only part of the picture. Transactions don't always map one-to-one. A single order can split into multiple settlement lines, while multiple orders can eventually settle into a single bank entry.

    On top of the reason, the shape of the records involved varies too. Transactions don't always map one-to-one — a single order can split into several settlement lines, or a batch settlement can bundle dozens of orders into one bank entry.

    →
    One-to-one
    A single order, a single settlement line. The expected case — and the one most tools assume by default.
    →
    One-to-many
    One order splits into several settlement entries — a capture, a fee, a refund — each landing separately.
    →
    Many-to-one
    Several orders settle together as a single bank entry — typically from end-of-day batching.
    →
    Many-to-many
    A batch of orders settles against a batch of bank entries, with no fixed pairing between them.

    An analyst isn't simply fixing mismatches—they're first reconstructing how records relate to one another.

    What happens next?

    A finance ops user reviews the exception and takes a resolution action:

    Edit entry  ·  Add entry  ·  Swap entry  ·  Delete entry  ·  Mark as expected

    /04 Where Analysts Get Stuck

    The exception label is only half the story.

    An exception comes with a topology label such as OneToOne or OneToMany. But that label only describes who is being compared, not what is actually happening underneath.

    Account-level topology — outer grouping
    Which OMS account(s) are being compared against which Adyen account(s). Tells you who's being compared — and determines how the system groups records before it can compare anything.
    OneToOne OneToMany ManyToOne ManyToMany
    Entry-level topology — nested inside
    Within that account pairing, how many individual transactions or entries exist on each side. Tells you what's being compared — and determines where the actual amount mismatch lives. Can vary independently of the account level above.
    OneToOne OneToMany ManyToOne ManyToMany

    Both layers can have completely different shapes, making the same exception appear deceptively simple.

    Why analysts get stuck

    • The exception label hides what's happening underneath.
    • Resolution depends on entry-level relationships, not account-level labels.
    • Analysts must reconstruct missing context manually.
    • The same mismatch can require entirely different actions.
    Design Challenge

    Make the hidden structure visible before investigation begins.

    The label alone isn't enough. The interface needs to reveal both who is being compared and what is actually being compared underneath.

    /05 Designing the Structure

    One structure, however the transaction splits.

    Designing a separate screen for every combination of mismatch reason and record shape would mean designing for all of the permutations above — and then some. Instead, we anchored the design around three things that hold true for every exception, no matter how many records sit on either side.

    01
    Context
    What was expected — pulled straight from the original transaction: order, amount, parties, timestamps.
    02
    Delta
    What's actually different — the one thing the analyst needs to see first, however many records it spans.
    03
    Action
    The resolution options valid for this exception's type — never a generic "edit" button.

    With this anchor in place, a one-to-many exception — one order, three settlement lines — and a many-to-many exception — fifty orders settling against one bank entry — can share the same screen. Only the Context and Delta panels resize to fit; the structure around them stays exactly where the analyst learned to expect it.
